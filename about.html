About

Objective:

The purpose of this document is to explain my approach to completing the following assignment:

“Please write a program that generates a list of 10,000 numbers in random order each time it is run. Each number in the list must be unique and be between 1 and 10,000 (inclusive).”

Analysis:

After careful consideration and extensive research on the subject, I found many seemingly viable solutions to this problem. The naïve approach would be to write a loop that generates random numbers from 1 through 10,000 and check to see whether or not they had already been generated. If not, add them to an output list. The main problem with this is efficiency.

This approach would be quick at first. However, it would become progressively more difficult to find a number that had not already been selected. After half of the numbers had been used, the odds are less than 50% of finding a good number. The odds of finding the final number in a list of 10,000 numbers are 1 in 10,000. This approach would take a long time to complete, which would provide a very poor user experience for the client.

My first goal was to ensure the accurate generation of the list of numbers. I wanted to make sure the list I created was completely random, with a uniform distribution, in the shortest time possible.

I realized that, since the numbers are unique and finite, what I ultimately had was the equivalent of a huge deck of cards. Therefore, I decided to use a shuffle approach. I used the Fisher-Yates Shuffle, because it produces a perfectly even distribution of possible solution sets. (Other shuffles can be biased towards some distributions, and are, therefore, not uniform.)

Solution

My solution was to use a “Durstenfeld Shuffle” algorithm. A well-known shuffle algorithm is the “Fisher-Yates Shuffle”, which was developed in 1938. This algorithm randomizes a set of increasing numbers. In 1964, Richard Durstenfeld published an algorithm that was based on the Fisher-Yates shuffle, but was optimized for computer use. The Durstenfeld Shuffle is much faster than the Fisher-Yates Shuffle.

Presentation

I am presenting the random/shuffled numbers in a table. The order of the numbers is from left to right, top to bottom.

I added pagination, in order to minimize the screen usage. The number of columns and pages will vary, depending on the screen size.

This application is optimized for responsive sites and mobile applications. When you resize your desktop browser, the table will adjust accordingly.
